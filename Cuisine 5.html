  • Phuchka(pronounsed Phush-ka,is a skinny piece of deep fried carbohydrate with a small hole to add some masala water)
  • Dal, a staple Indian dish of dried, split pulses, has roots dating back over 5,000 years to the Indus Valley Civilization (c. 2500 BCE). Historically, it was a staple food mentioned in ancient Ayurvedic texts and Vedas for its nutritional value. The term derives from Sanskrit, meaning "to split".
  • Puri is an ancient unleavened, deep-fried Indian bread originating in the Indian subcontinent (likely Northern India) during the Vedic period. Made from flour and water, it has been a staple breakfast and festive food for centuries, historically documented in early cookbooks and literature. It is iconic for its puffy, golden appearance.
  • Scouse(an unknow dish of of Great Britain) is a hearty, slow-cooked stew of meat (usually beef or lamb) and root vegetables, central to Liverpool’s history as a 19th-century port city. It originated from Scandinavian lobscouse, a sailor’s dish of salted meat and hardtack biscuit, which became a staple for the poor and working class, evolving into a symbol of Liverpudlian identity and resilience.
  • The Sunday roast is a traditional British meal originating from medieval times, solidified in the 15th-century reign of King Henry VII. It was originally a large, celebratory post-church meal following the fasting of Sunday morning services. It was famously perfected in the 1700s, with families leaving meat in the oven to cook while they attended church.
  • The samosa originated in Central Asia/Middle East before the 10th century, likely as a baked, meat-filled pastry called sanbosag. It travelled to India via trade routes around the 13th-14th century, where it evolved into a fried, vegetarian-friendly, triangular snack. The iconic potato filling became popular much later following the 18th-century introduction of potatoes.
  • Dosa is a popular South Indian savory crepe made from a fermented batter of rice and black gram (urad dal), known for its crispy texture and tangy flavor. Originating centuries ago, this staple breakfast dish is traditionally served hot with sambar (lentil stew) and various chutneys. High in protein and carbohydrates, it is both nutritious and versatile, with popular variations including the potato-stuffed masala Dosa.
